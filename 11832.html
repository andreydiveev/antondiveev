Look at Me

December 1, 2015Darcy

As we enter the ‘party season’, when many a selfie will taken, shared and liked, Carlotta Buosi explores the effects of new age narcissism.

Illustration by Yasmin Rahal

Narcissus is one of the most famous figures in Greek mythology. A handsome man, he was punished by the gods for falling in love with his own image. The myth of Narcissus has been endlessly revisited in art and inspired a personality disorder: narcissism. But if Narcissus’s story will remain impressed in literature exactly as it was written, narcissism seems to be continuously evolving, especially in the hyper-technological era in which we live.

According to recent studies, 30% of young Americans are classifiable as narcissists, a number which has doubled in the last thirty years with a consequent decline of the average level of empathy – the attitude which is classified as the opposite of narcissism – by 40 percent since 1980. What is it that is making the younger generation so prone to narcissism?

Illustration by Yasmin Rahali

Social media takes a good deal of the blame by turning everyone into likes-obsessed and followers-seekers on Twitter and Instagram. Selfies have rapidly become a dangerous tool of self-involvement, whose consequences are still to be defined. Among them, for example, the rise of plastic surgery. A survey by the American Academy of Facial Plastic and Reconstructive Surgery of a pool of 2700 respondents reported an increase of 10% in practices such as rhinoplasty, hair transplants and eyelid surgery from 2012 to 2013 due to the patients’ social media awareness.

The word selfie was given an official definition in 2013 and inserted in the Oxford Dictionary, while the number of times the word was mentioned on Instagram – the biggest selfie playground among all socials – raised to over 34 billions tags. The number is constantly rising thanks to a crowd of afficiandos who’ve let selfies enter their daily routines, but also due to the spreading of this addictive phenomenon among celebrities. The undisputed queen of selfies is Kim Kardashian West, who has created such a wide portfolio of pictures of herself taken by herself that publishing house Rizzoli International considered it worthy of publication. That’s how Selfish was born: a 352 page book of photographs which might be the turning point of this phenomenon, turning selfies into a official form of – if not art – entertainment.

Illustration by Yasmin Rahali

What’s most dangerous about the contribution of social media and self-based platforms such as blogs – especially fashion blogs – to a self-involved culture is that they’re rapidly helping to fade the line between a healthy bit of vanity and actual narcissism.

In a society that, in most cases, celebrates self expression and freedom of speech, who are we to disagree? That might be the reason why some of the most successful self doers have managed not only to behave just as they would if they didn’t have a camera pointing at them, but also to make a living out of it, being fed their own narcissism by the media. Bryanboy, the Filippino blogger who named his fashion platform after himself, is just one example of the many bloggers being given a free pass into a glamorous reality which feed their narcissistic nature. Being rewarded for behaving yourself might be a source of great satisfaction, but if blogging and sharing content should imply the necessity of communicating a message, the only one these bloggers are eager to showcase is a fine selection of themselves at their best. Looking richer, thinner and more stylish in every picture published is the goal, ultimately loosing the desire of interacting with the audience and the approachable attitude which used to be a key feature of bloggers rather than journalists.

Freud said that everyone of us has a healthy percentage of narcissism. Loving yourself is not a crime but in today’s world, dominated by bloggers and selfie-takers, technology and media are playing a key role in turning self-love in an unhealthy obsession with yourself, along with the necessity of creating something out of yourself which is perfect. Long series of beautiful photographs portray what the bloggers call everyday life, which often has very little to do with real life. It is unrealistic, and yet blogging continues to expand.
